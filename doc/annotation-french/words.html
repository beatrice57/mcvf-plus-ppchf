AINS, AINZ

When it means EARLIER, AINS is tagged ADVR.

When it means RATHER (common in contrastive environments), AINS is tagged as simple ADV.

When construed with a QUE clause, AINS is treated as part of the subordinate clause. When AINS and QUE are discontinuous, AINS is annotated as having undergone leftward movement, as is our default. 

(CP-ADV (ADV ains)
        (CONJS que)
	(IP-SUB (NP-SBJ (D le) (NCS coq))
  	        (VJ chantast)))

(IP-MAT (ADVP-1 (ADV ains))
         ...
        (CP-ADV (ADVP *ICH*-1)
                (CONJS que)
	        (IP-SUB (NP-SBJ (D le) (NCS cooq))
  	                (VJ chantast))))

CL versus PRO

Subject pronouns are always tagged PRO. Object pronouns, on the other hand, are sometimes tagged as ordinary pronouns (PRO) and sometimes as clitics (CL). This is in contrast to the Ottawa annotation conventions, which do not use the CL tag.

There are in fact two relevant distinctions: a prosodic one, and a syntactic one. Prosodically, pro-forms can be weak (me, te, le, se, les) or strong (also known as tonic) (moi, toi, soi, eux). Syntactically, pro-forms can be required to be adjacent to their licensing head, or they can have the relatively free distribution of ordinary noun phrases. In principle, this gives rise to four expected possibilities, which are annotated as follows:

Prosody Syntax Tag Example(s)
Weak Separable This combination doesn't occur.
Nonseparable (preverbal) CL Le roi le/CL m'/CL a donné.
Strong Nonseparable (postverbal in imperatives and other verb-initial contexts) CL Donne le/CL moi/CL
Separable (conjunction contexts, complement of preposition, left-dislocation) PRO Le roi a invité ton frère et toi/PRO.
Le roi a invité toi/PRO et ton frère.
Moi/PRO , je l' aime.
Ce chien est à moi/PRO.

To complicate matters yet a bit further, some forms are prosodically ambiguous (lui, nous, vous). Such forms are annotated as CL or PRO depending on the syntactic context.

Le roi lui/CL a donné un grand chien. (preverbal clitic)
Ce chien est a nous/PRO (complement of preposition)
Vous/PRO , vous êtes fou. (left-dislocation)

COMME

The treatment of COMME is similar to that of QUE. Possible tags are CONJS, P, WADV.
  • COMME is most commonly tagged as the CONJS head of an adverbial clause (CP-ADV), possibly involving elision. When required by the meaning, the CP-ADV dominates a silent wh- pronoun, mostly commonly WADVP. 
    (CP-ADV (CONJS comme)			← causal interpretation
        (IP-SUB (NP-SBJ (PRO elle))
		(VJ est)
		(ADJP-PRD (ADJ jolie))))

(CP-ADV (WADVP-1 0)			← manner interpretation requires silent wh- adverbial
        (CONJS comme)
        (IP-SUB (ADVP *T*-1)
                (NP-SBJ (PRO j'))
		(AJ ai)
		(VPP dit)))

(NP (D un) (NCS homme)			← elision + wh- pronoun
    (CP-ADV (WADJP-1 0)
	    (CONJS comme)
	    (IP-SUB (ADJP-PRD *T*-1)
		    (NP-SBJ (DZ mon) (NCS mari)))))
  • In connection with SI, COMME is tagged as the head of a comparative clause. 
    (ADVP (ADV si)
      (CP-CMP (WADVP-1 0)		← comparative, not adverbial clause
	      (CONJS comme)
              (IP-SUB (ADVP *T*-1)
		      (NP-SBJ (PROIMP il))
		      (VJ faut))))
  • COMME can function as the CONJS head of a complement clause (CP-THT). 
    (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ (PRO Elle))
	(VJ dit)
	(CP-THT (CONJS comme)
		(IP-SUB ...)))
  • COMME is tagged as a preposition (P) when a full clausal structure is difficult or impossible to implement. 
    (IP-INF (VX nommer)
	(NP-ACC (Q quelqu'un))
	(PP (P comme)
	    (NP (NCS président))))
  • Finally, COMME can be tagged as a wh- adverb when it modifies adjectives or adverbs. 
    (CP-EXL (WADJP-1 (WADV comme) (ADJ grands))
        (IP-SUB (ADJP *T*-1)
		(VJ sont)
		(NP-SBJ (D les) (NCPL dents)
			(PP (P d@)
			    (NP (D @u) (NCS loup))))))

ET

ET can be:
  • a coordinating conjunction (CONJO)
  • the 2nd ps sg. of the verb ÊTRE (EJ, VJ)
  • the Latin etymon of the conjunction (ETR),
  • an orthographic variant of the interjection ES, which in the absence of a verb projects IP-PRES
  • an adverb-like item currently tagged X

Concessive or focus-like functions of ET are generally treated as instances of gapping. 

( (IP-MAT (IP-MAT-1 (NP-SBJ (PRO Elle))

FAIRE BIEN / MAL

In connection with FAIRE, BIEN and MAL can be tagged either as adverbs (ADV) or as nouns (NCS), depending on the meaning. The distinction is that between acting correctly or wisely (DO WELL) versus doing good deeds (DO GOOD). Both preverbal and postverbal BIEN and MAL are possible with either meaning. In doubtful cases, the default is ADV.

FAIRE QUE FOL / PREU / SAGE

In this locution, QUE corresponds to COMME and is treated as the CONJS head of an elided adverbial clause. 
(IP-INF (VX faire)
        (CP-ADV (WADVP-1 0)
	        (CONJS que)
	        (IP-SUB (ADVP *T*-1)
		        (NP-SBJ (NCS fol / preu / sage)))))

In older stages of French, Y can be missing. The postverbal noun phrase is treated as the associate of the impersonal subject (rather than as a direct object).

When IL Y A introduces temporal expressions, it is integrated into the matrix structure as follows: 

( (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ (PRO elle))
          (EJ est)
          (VPP venue)

			  (IP-INF (NP-ACC *T*-1)
				  (VX faire)
				  (PP (P de)

					      (PP (P en)
						  (NP (NCS mesnage))))))))
          (PONFP .)))

( (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ (PRO Je))

The treatment of QUE is similar to that of COMME. Possible tags are: CONJO, CONJS, FP, P, PR, WPRO, X.
  • The most common use of QUE is as a subordinating conjunction (CONJS) heading complement clauses (CP-THT), comparative clauses (CP-CMP), degree clauses (CP-DEG), adverbial clauses (CP-ADV), and others. 
    (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ (DZ Mon) (NCS ami))
        (AJ a)
	(VPP dit)
	(CP-THT (CONJS qu')
                (IP-SUB (NP-SBJ (PRO il))
		        (VJ viendra))))

(NP (Q tant)
    (CP-CMP (WNP-1 0)
            (CONJS que)			← see Comparative clauses for more details
	    (IP-SUB (NP-SBJ (PRO on))
	            (MDJ peut)
		    (IP-INF (NP-ACC *T*-1)
			    (VX faire)))))
  • Causal QUE (corresponding to French CAR, English FOR, or German DENN) is treated as a coordinating conjunction (CONJO). This use is particularly common in older texts.

    Another possible use of QUE (or QUI) as a coordinating conjunction, again primarily in older texts, is as a correlative conjunction. 

    ( (NP (CONJO que)
      (NCS chevalier)
      (CONJP (CONJO que)
	     (NCS dame))))

( (NP (CONJO qui)
      (NCS chevalier)
      (CONJP (CONJO qui)
	     (NCS dame))))

  • In indirect questions, relative clauses, and similar constructions, QUE can function as a wh- pronoun (WPRO). By default, QUE in comparative clauses is treated as a subordinating conjunction (CONJS), not as a wh- pronoun (WPRO). 
    (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ (PRO Je))
        (NEG ne)
        (VJ sais)
        (CP-QUE (WNP-1 (WPRO que))
                (IP-INF (NP-ACC *T*-1)
			(VX faire))))
		    
(NP (D la) (NCS fille)
    (CP-REL (WNP-1 (WPRO que))
            (IP-SUB (NP-ACC *T*-1)
		    (NP-SBJ (PRO je))
		    (AJ ai)
		    (VPP vue))))
  • QUE is tagged as a comparative preposition (PR) when a full clausal comparative is difficult or impossible to implement. This use is essentially restricted to instances of QUE licensed by AUTRE or governing a clause or phrase headed by DE. 
    (NP (ADJ aultres) (NCPL pechiez)
    (PP (PR que)
        (ADJP (ADJ mortels))))

(ADJP (ADV si) (ADJ fol)
      (PP-DEG (PR que)
              (PP (P de)
                  (IP-INF (CL-NP-ACC (CL la))
                          (VX battre)))))
  • QUE is tagged as a focus particle (FP) in conjunction with sentence negation when the force of the sentence is positive. 
    (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ (PRO Je))
        (NEG n')
        (VJ ai)
        (NP-ACC (FP que) (PRO toi)))

    The historical source of the FP use is presumably the PR use with an empty nominal head (I do not have other than you = I have only you).

  • In exceptional cases, QUE can be tagged as an ordinary preposition (P). 
    (NP-MSR (PP (P d')
	    (ADVP (ADV ici)))
        (PP (P qu')
	    (PP (P en)
		(NP (NCS Orient)))))
  • Some uses of QUE are so mysterious that they are tagged as X. See also TANT QUE ... ET. 
    (IP-MAT (NP-SBJ=1 (PRO C'))
        (VJ est)
	(NP-PRD (Q peu)
	        (NP-PART (DF de) (NCS chose)))
        (NP-1 (X que) (DF d@) (DF @u) (NCS peuple)))

(CP-CAR (WNP-2 (WPRO qui))
	       (IP-SUB (NP-SBJ=3 *T*-2)
		       (VJ est)
		       (NP-PRD (D une)
			       (ADJ belle)
			       (NCS allegacion))
		        (IP-INF-3 (X que) (VX alleguer)
				  (NP-ACC (D le) (NCS cas)
					  (CP-REL (WNP-4 (WPRO que))
						  (IP-SUB (NP-SBJ (PRO l'en))
							  (AJ a)
							  (VPP veu)
							  (IP-INF (NP-SBJ *T*-4)
								  (VX avenir))))))))

SI

For the treatment of SI COMME, see COMME.

When introducing adverbial clauses or indirect questions clauses, SI is tagged CONJS. The distinction between the two clause types is encoded in the dash tag of the relevant clause (CP-ADV vs. CP-QUE).

SI can also be tagged ADV. In older French, this use of SI is very common, particularly in the topic position of V2 clauses. In contrast to AUSSI and spelling variants, semantically bleached SI is not treated as resumptive when preceded by a potentially left-dislocated constituent.

Finally, SI can be an interjection (ITJ) - the affirmative counterpart of NON.

SI can also a spelling variant of various other forms:

  • the plural possessive pronoun SES (DZ)
  • the numeral SIX (ADJNUM or NUM)
  • the pronoun CEUX (PRO)

TANT ... QUE ...

When TANT ... QUE corresponds to a bipartitite coordination structure, it is annotated as such rather than as an elliptical comparative structure (in contrast to the current guidelines concerning the AS WELL ... AS construction in the English corpora, which will be assimilated to the French guidelines in the next release). 

(PP (CONJO tant)
    (PP (P en)
	(IP-PPL (VG dormant)))
    (CONJP (CONJO comme)
           (PP (P en)
	       (IP-PPL (NP-ACC (ADJ aultres) (NCPL choses))
		       (VG faisant)))))

(PP (CONJO tant)
    (PP (P par)
        (NP (D le) (NCS bois)))
    (CONJP (CONJO comme)
           (PP (P e@)
	       (NP (D @s) (NPCL pres)))))

Otherwise (when TANT ... QUE corresponds to the meanings just discussed), TANT is annotated as having undergone leftward movement, as is our default. 

(IP-MAT (QP-1 (Q tant))
         ...
        (NP-MSR (QP *ICH*-1)
                (CP-CMP ... )))

(IP-MAT (ADVP-1 (ADV tant))
         ...
        (CP-ADV (ADVP *ICH*-1)
                ...))

In a late variant, QUE can be replaced by ET, and TANT is sometimes followed by QUE, which is treated as expletive. 

(PP (CONJO tant)
    (X que)
    (PP (P par)
        (NP (D le) (NCS bois)))
    (CONJP (CONJO et)
           (PP (P e@)
	       (NP (D @s) (NPCL pres)))))
